Nitriding Process

Surface hardening process that introduces nitrogen to form hard nitride compounds, achieving exceptional wear resistance without dimensional change

What is Nitriding?

Nitriding is a thermochemical surface treatment process where nitrogen is diffused into the surface of steel at temperatures below the critical temperature. This process forms hard nitride compounds in the surface layer, significantly improving wear resistance, fatigue strength, and corrosion resistance.

Temperature Range

500-580°C (below critical temperature)

Atmosphere

Ammonia (NH₃) or nitrogen-bearing compounds

Process Time

10-100 hours depending on case depth required

Types of Nitriding

Different nitriding methods offer varying process characteristics and applications

Gas Nitriding

Traditional method using ammonia gas atmosphere for nitrogen diffusion.

Atmosphere: NH₃ + H₂
Temperature: 500-580°C
Time: 20-100 hours
Case Depth: 0.1-0.7 mm

Plasma Nitriding

Advanced method using ionized nitrogen in a glow discharge atmosphere.

Atmosphere: N₂ + H₂ plasma
Temperature: 350-580°C
Time: 4-20 hours
Case Depth: 0.05-0.5 mm

Salt Bath Nitriding

Liquid nitriding using molten salt baths containing nitrogen-bearing compounds.

Medium: Molten salts
Temperature: 520-580°C
Time: 1-6 hours
Case Depth: 0.01-0.3 mm

Effects of Alloying Elements

Alloying elements significantly influence nitriding behavior and the properties of the nitrided layer

Nitride-Forming Elements

Al

Aluminum

Forms AlN - extremely hard nitrides, highest surface hardness

Cr

Chromium

Forms CrN - stable nitrides, good wear resistance

V

Vanadium

Forms VN - very hard, fine precipitation strengthening

Ti

Titanium

Forms TiN - golden color, high hardness and thermal stability

Other Alloying Elements

Molybdenum (Mo): Refines nitride distribution
Nickel (Ni): Reduces nitriding kinetics
Manganese (Mn): Forms Mn₄N nitrides
Silicon (Si): Minimal nitride formation

Hardness vs Alloying Elements

Note: Aluminum and Vanadium provide the highest surface hardness increases

Application: Nitralloy steels contain 1% Al for optimal nitriding response

Microstructure and Properties

Understanding the nitrided layer structure and resulting mechanical properties

Layer Structure

Compound Layer (White Layer)

Outer layer containing Fe₂₋₃N and Fe₄N phases, 5-25 μm thick

Diffusion Zone

Subsurface layer with nitrogen in solid solution and nitride precipitates

Core Material

Unaffected base metal with original properties

Industrial Applications

Nitriding is essential for components requiring exceptional surface properties

Automotive

Crankshafts, camshafts, valve components, and gear systems

Aerospace

Landing gear components, turbine parts, and precision mechanisms

Tooling

Dies, molds, cutting tools, and precision instruments

Industrial

Hydraulic components, pump parts, and wear-resistant surfaces
