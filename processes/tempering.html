Tempering Process

Secondary heat treatment that reduces brittleness while maintaining hardness, optimizing the balance between strength and toughness

What is Tempering?

Tempering is a heat treatment process performed after quenching to reduce the brittleness of martensite while maintaining acceptable hardness levels. The process involves reheating the quenched steel to temperatures below the critical temperature, followed by controlled cooling.

Temperature Range

150-650°C depending on desired properties

Holding Time

1-4 hours for complete transformation

Cooling Method

Air cooling or furnace cooling

Tempering Stages

Tempering occurs in distinct stages, each characterized by specific microstructural changes and property modifications

I

First Stage

Precipitation of ε-carbide from supersaturated martensite.

Temperature: 80-200°C
Carbide: ε-carbide
Effect: Slight softening
II

Second Stage

Decomposition of retained austenite to bainite.

Temperature: 200-300°C
Product: Bainite
Effect: Hardness increase
III

Third Stage

Formation of cementite and recovery of martensite.

Temperature: 300-400°C
Carbide: Fe₃C
Effect: Significant softening
IV

Fourth Stage

Coarsening of cementite particles and recrystallization.

Temperature: 400-700°C
Process: Coarsening
Effect: Further softening

Secondary Hardening

Special phenomenon in alloy steels where hardness increases during tempering at specific temperatures

Mechanism

Alloy Carbide Precipitation

Formation of fine, hard carbides (Mo₂C, V₄C₃, Cr₂₃C₆)

Precipitation Hardening

Fine carbides act as obstacles to dislocation movement

Temperature Range

Typically occurs between 500-600°C

Affected Steel Types

  • Tool steels (H13, D2, M2)
  • High-speed steels
  • Maraging steels
  • Some alloy steels with Mo, V, Cr

Secondary Hardening Curve

Peak: Maximum secondary hardening at ~550°C

Application: Tool steels for high-temperature service

Industrial Applications

Tempering is crucial for achieving optimal properties in various engineering applications

Automotive Springs

Leaf and coil springs requiring high strength and fatigue resistance

Hand Tools

Chisels, punches, and hammers needing toughness with hardness

Machine Components

Shafts, gears, and bearings requiring balanced properties

Structural Steel

Construction components requiring ductility and strength
